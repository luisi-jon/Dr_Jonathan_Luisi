History as A Chemical Warfare Agent

Phosgene was used extensively as a choking agent during World War I, accounting for the majority of gas-related fatalities. It is currently strictly monitored under the Chemical Weapons Convention (CWC) as a Schedule 3 precursor. Facilities producing or consuming significant quantities must report to relevant national and international authorities (e.g., OPCW).

General Description

Phosgene is a highly toxic, colorless gas at room temperature with a characteristic odor of newly mown hay or green corn at low concentrations. It is a vital industrial intermediate primarily used in the production of polyurethanes, polycarbonates, and pesticides.

Handling & Storage

Must be handled in a certified chemical fume hood or gas cabinet. Use only with dedicated piping systems. Avoid all skin and eye contact. Do not breathe gas or vapors. Personnel should be trained in emergency procedures and the use of detection equipment.

Storage Instructions

Store in a cool, dry, well-ventilated area designated for toxic gases. Keep cylinders upright and secured. Protect from sunlight and moisture. Separate from incompatible materials like ammonia, amines, and alkali metals.

Research & Medical Insights

Therapeutic Aspects

"Several therapies were able to significantly increase 24 hr survival following an LCt50–70 exposure to phosgene... these studies provide evidence that mortality following phosgene toxicity can be mitigated by neuro- and calcium-regulators, antioxidants, phosphodiesterase and endothelin receptor antagonists."

Research into medical countermeasures for phosgene (COCl2) inhalation suggests that while no universal antidote exists, survival can be improved through the administration of antioxidants and signaling pathway inhibitors. Current therapeutic research focuses on mitigating the inflammatory response and vascular permeability triggered by exposure.

Clinical management of phosgene poisoning primarily relies on supportive care. Standard treatments include oxygen therapy, non-invasive or invasive mechanical ventilation, and the use of bronchodilators to manage acute respiratory distress and pulmonary edema.

Toxicological Aspects

"Phosgene appears as a chemical warfare choking agent. Extremely toxic. A colorless gas or very low-boiling, volatile liquid with an odor of new-mown hay or green corn. Warning properties of the gas inhaled are slight, death may occur within 36 hours."

Phosgene (Chemical Formula: COCl2, PubChem CID: 6371) is a potent pulmonary irritant. It is highly reactive with biological nucleophiles in the lungs, leading to deep tissue damage and delayed pulmonary edema. Warfare Hazard Status: warfare. Disclaimer: Phosgene is a Schedule 3 substance under the Chemical Weapons Convention; its production and transfer are strictly regulated internationally due to its history as a lethal chemical weapon.

Reference Link →
"In its first application, phosgene was the most heavily used chemical-warfare agent during World War I... phosgene at concentrations as low as 0.5 ppm for 2 h caused definitive pathologic changes in the lungs of rats."

Toxicological studies confirm phosgene's high lethality at low concentrations. Historically, it was first deployed by France in 1915 during WWI and was responsible for approximately 85,000 deaths (roughly 85% of chemical warfare fatalities in that conflict) due to its insidious nature and delayed onset of symptoms.

Reference Link →
"The toxic effects of phosgene are not due to its hydrolysis and conversion to HCl... Phosgene causes non-cardiogenic pulmonary oedema in humans and increases lipid peroxidation and vascular permeability."

The mechanism of phosgene toxicity involves acylation of alveolar proteins and lipids rather than simple acid hydrolysis. This process disrupts the blood-air barrier, causing fluid to leak into the lungs, leading to non-cardiogenic pulmonary edema and eventual asphyxiation.

History, News & Regulatory Milestones

Historical Summary

Phosgene (COCl2) was first synthesized in 1812 by John Davy through the reaction of carbon monoxide and chlorine in sunlight. It gained international infamy during World War I as a highly lethal choking agent, responsible for approximately 85% of chemical warfare fatalities. Following the war, phosgene evolved into a cornerstone of the global chemical industry, serving as a vital intermediate in the production of polyurethanes, polycarbonates, and pharmaceuticals. Due to its extreme toxicity and history as a weapon, it is strictly monitored under the Chemical Weapons Convention as a Schedule 3 substance. Recent developments have focused on strengthening industrial safety protocols (EPA RMP updates) and responding to accidental leaks at manufacturing facilities.

1812History

Scientific Discovery of Phosgene

British chemist John Davy first synthesizes phosgene by exposing a mixture of carbon monoxide and chlorine to sunlight. He named it from the Greek words 'phos' (light) and 'gene' (born).

October 1915History

First Military Deployment of Phosgene

Phosgene is first used as a chemical weapon by the French army. Shortly after, in December 1915, the German army deployed phosgene in a combined chlorine-phosgene attack at Wieltje, Belgium.

June 17, 1925Regulatory

The Geneva Protocol

The 'Protocol for the Prohibition of the Use in War of Asphyxiating, Poisonous or Other Gases' is signed, banning the use of phosgene and other chemical agents in international armed conflicts.

April 29, 1997Regulatory

Chemical Weapons Convention (CWC) Entry into Force

The CWC enters into force, classifying phosgene as a Schedule 3 substance. This requires facilities producing more than 30 tonnes per year to declare their activities to the OPCW.

January 2021Regulatory

ACGIH Phosgene Exposure Limit Update

The American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists (ACGIH) updates its threshold limit values, lowering the Ceiling (TLV-C) for phosgene to 0.02 ppm to better protect workers.

December 4, 2023News

Altivia Chemicals Phosgene Leak

A ruptured pressure release device at the Altivia Chemicals plant in La Porte, Texas, causes a phosgene leak. Eight workers are hospitalized and a shelter-in-place order is issued for the community.

March 11, 2024Regulatory

EPA Finalizes 'Safer Communities by Chemical Accident Prevention' Rule

The U.S. EPA publishes a final rule amending the Risk Management Program (RMP). The rule requires chemical plants using toxic substances like phosgene to conduct safer technology and alternatives analyses.

November 27, 2024News

Suspected Phosgene Leak at Tagoor Laboratories

An industrial accident at Tagoor Laboratories in Anakapalli, India, results in a gas release. Scientific advocacy groups suspect phosgene was involved, leading to investigations by local pollution boards.
